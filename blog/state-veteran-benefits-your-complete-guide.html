State Veteran Benefits: Your Complete Guide

Federal VA benefits get most of the attention, but the benefits your state offers veterans are often underutilized — and in some cases, more immediately valuable. Every state administers its own veteran benefit programs, and the variation is enormous. A veteran in Texas may be eligible for a full property tax exemption, free college tuition for dependents, and a state hiring preference. A veteran in Florida may qualify for a different combination that includes a homestead exemption, professional license fee waivers, and state employment points. This guide covers the major categories and how to find what applies to you.

Property Tax Exemptions

Property tax exemptions for veterans are available in virtually every state, but the eligibility requirements and amounts vary widely. Some states offer partial exemptions based on disability rating — veterans with a 10% or higher rating may qualify for a reduced exemption, while veterans with 100% service-connected disability often qualify for a full property tax exemption. In states like Texas, Virginia, and Florida, the 100% exemption can represent tens of thousands of dollars in annual savings.

The exemption typically requires an application through your county assessor or tax commissioner's office. Most require proof of your disability rating from the VA. If you're rated 100% and not claiming this exemption, you're leaving significant money on the table every year. Contact your state's Department of Veterans Affairs or county tax office to apply.

Education Benefits for Veterans and Dependents

Many states offer tuition waivers or reduced tuition at state colleges and universities for veterans and their dependents. Some of these programs are exceptionally generous. In several states, dependents of veterans who died in service or are rated 100% service-connected can attend state universities for free or at significantly reduced cost. The GI Bill handles federal education benefits, but state programs are often stackable — you can potentially use both.

State-funded education benefits are administered by state agencies and are entirely separate from the VA. Check your state's Department of Veterans Affairs website, or look up your state's Higher Education Agency to find what education programs are available for veterans and their families.

Employment and Hiring Preferences

Most states offer veterans' preference in state government hiring — the same concept as federal veterans' preference, but applied to state agency jobs. The way this works varies: some states add a set number of points to civil service exam scores, others require agencies to interview all qualified veteran applicants, and some require agencies to give veterans priority consideration over non-veteran candidates with equivalent qualifications.

Some states extend this preference to disabled veterans at a higher level — meaning a veteran with a service-connected disability may have a stronger preference than a non-disabled veteran. If you're pursuing state government employment, understand the preference rules in your state before you apply. They can meaningfully affect your competitiveness.

Small Business and Entrepreneurship Programs

Several states operate veteran-specific small business programs that include reduced licensing fees, procurement preferences (set-asides for veteran-owned businesses on state contracts), business development training, and in some cases, grant funding. These programs are distinct from federal SBA programs and operate through state economic development agencies or state-level veterans service departments.

If you're starting or running a business, verify whether your state requires separate veteran-owned business certification (most do) and what procurement advantages it provides. State contracts can be significant — and veteran business certifications are often undersought.

Healthcare and Mental Health Programs

Some states fund veteran-specific healthcare programs that supplement or provide alternatives to VA care. State veterans homes — residential care facilities funded at the state level — are available in most states and provide nursing home, domiciliary, and adult day health care. Several states also fund community-based mental health programs specifically for veterans, including programs designed to address gaps in VA mental health access. A few states maintain state-funded veteran healthcare programs entirely independent of the VA.

How to Find Your State's Programs

The most reliable starting point is your state's Department of Veterans Affairs website — every state has one. The National Association of State Veterans Homes (NASVH) maintains a directory. The Military Officers Association of America (MOAA) and state-level VSOs like the American Legion and VFW state departments often maintain current benefit guides for their states. The National Conference of State Legislatures tracks state veterans legislation and publishes accessible summaries. Our State Benefits resource hub organizes key programs by state in a searchable format.

Actionable Takeaway

Open your state's Department of Veterans Affairs website today and review the full benefit list. Specifically check: property tax exemption eligibility, any education benefits for you or your dependents, and whether state employment hiring preference applies to positions you're targeting. If you're rated 100% service-connected and own property, prioritize the property tax exemption application — it is typically the highest annual dollar value of any state benefit and takes one application to activate.
