How to File a VA Disability Claim: A Step-by-Step Guide

The VA disability claims process has a reputation for being slow, confusing, and frustrating. That reputation is earned. But it's navigable — if you know the steps, build your evidence correctly from the start, and understand what the VA is actually looking for. This guide walks you through the process in plain language.

Step 1: Determine What You're Claiming

Before you file anything, build a list of every condition you believe is connected to your military service. This includes physical injuries, illnesses, and mental health conditions. Do not self-censor. Veterans frequently underreport because they don't believe something is "bad enough" to claim — the VA will make that determination. Your job is to put every legitimate service-connected condition on the table.

For each condition, you need to be able to establish: (1) a current diagnosis, (2) an in-service event, injury, or exposure that caused or contributed to it, and (3) a medical connection between the two. This is called the "nexus." Without all three elements, a claim is likely to be denied.

Step 2: Gather Your Evidence Before You File

The most common reason claims are delayed or denied is insufficient evidence. Gather these before submitting: your service records (DD-214 and service treatment records), private medical records documenting your conditions, buddy statements from fellow service members who witnessed incidents or your deterioration, and if possible, a nexus letter from a private physician connecting your condition to service.

The VA will request records from military facilities directly, but private records are your responsibility. Many veterans file without private records and wait months for VA exams that produce weaker opinions than a well-documented private physician letter.

Step 3: File Through eBenefits, VA.gov, or a VSO

You can file online at VA.gov, in person at a VA regional office, by mail, or through a Veterans Service Organization (VSO). Using a VSO — organizations like the DAV, VFW, American Legion, or AMVETS — is free and strongly recommended. VSO representatives are accredited and can help you build your claim correctly before you submit. A poorly structured initial claim creates a longer path to the right rating.

When you submit, you'll file VA Form 21-526EZ. This is the standard application for disability compensation. You can file an Intent to File first, which locks your effective date while you gather evidence — this protects your back pay.

Step 4: Attend Your C&P Exam

After submission, the VA will schedule a Compensation and Pension (C&P) exam with a VA physician or contract examiner. This exam is critical — the examiner's opinion heavily influences your rating. Attend every scheduled exam without exception. Be thorough and honest about your worst days, not your average days. Describe how the condition affects your work, sleep, relationships, and daily function. Veterans frequently understate symptoms at these exams out of habit; do not do that here.

Step 5: Understand the Rating Decision and Your Options

After the exam, the VA will issue a rating decision. You'll receive a letter explaining the decision, the percentage assigned to each condition, and the combined rating. If you disagree with any part of the decision, you have one year to appeal. The three appeal options under the Appeals Modernization Act are: Supplemental Claim (new evidence), Higher-Level Review (different VA employee reviews the same record), or Board of Veterans Appeals (a Veterans Law Judge hears your case).

Don't let a denial be the end. A large percentage of denials are successfully appealed with proper documentation and representation.

Actionable Takeaway

File an Intent to File today at VA.gov to lock your effective date, then spend 30–60 days gathering private medical records and buddy statements before submitting the full claim. The strongest claims are built before submission, not patched together during appeals.
