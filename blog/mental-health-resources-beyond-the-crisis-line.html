Mental Health Resources Beyond the Crisis Line

The Veterans Crisis Line (988, Press 1) is a lifeline — and it saves lives. But crisis intervention is not the same as ongoing mental health support. Most veterans don't need a crisis line most of the time. They need consistent, accessible support for PTSD, depression, anxiety, moral injury, and the everyday weight of carrying a veteran's experience through a civilian world that rarely understands it. This guide covers what's available beyond the emergency moment.

VA Mental Health Services: What You're Actually Entitled To

If you have VA healthcare eligibility, you have access to mental health services at no cost — including individual therapy, group therapy, medication management, and intensive outpatient programs. You do not need a service-connected disability rating to receive VA mental health care. Any veteran who served on active duty and was discharged under conditions other than dishonorable can enroll in VA healthcare, and mental health care is available to all enrolled veterans.

VA facilities offer evidence-based treatments specifically validated for veterans: Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT) and Prolonged Exposure (PE) for PTSD, and problem-solving therapy for depression. If your local VA has long wait times, ask about the Community Care Program — it allows you to see a VA-approved private therapist at no cost when VA capacity is limited.

Vet Centers: Lower Barrier, High Quality

Vet Centers are VA-funded community-based counseling centers staffed primarily by veterans. They're designed for veterans who want to talk to someone who's been there — without walking through a large hospital complex. Vet Centers provide individual counseling, couples counseling, family therapy, and substance use counseling. They're walk-in friendly, often in off-VA locations, and the barrier to entry is deliberately low. There are over 300 Vet Centers across the country. You can find yours at va.gov/find-locations.

Peer Support Programs

Peer support — being matched with another veteran who has navigated similar experiences — is one of the most effective interventions for veterans who distrust traditional clinical settings. Several programs operate at scale. Give an Hour connects veterans with licensed mental health professionals volunteering their time. The Headstrong Project provides free mental health treatment specifically for post-9/11 veterans and their families through partnered clinicians. The Veterans' and Military Families' Mental Health Initiative (VAMFHI) funds community-based programs across the country.

Within the VA system, Peer Specialists — veterans in recovery who are trained and certified to support other veterans — are embedded in many facilities. Ask your VA care team if peer specialist services are available at your location.

Digital Tools and Apps

The VA has developed several mobile apps specifically for veteran mental health that are evidence-based and free. PTSD Coach provides education, self-assessment tools, and coping strategies. The Mindfulness Coach app offers guided exercises validated for stress and anxiety management. Insomnia Coach addresses sleep disruption — one of the most common and undertreated veteran mental health issues. Moving Forward teaches problem-solving skills for stress. These aren't substitutes for therapy, but they provide support between sessions and for veterans who aren't yet ready to engage with formal care.

When the VA Doesn't Work for You

Not every veteran has a positive VA experience, and there are legitimate reasons some veterans seek care entirely outside the system. Private therapists who specialize in trauma and veteran populations are available through several directories: the Psychology Today therapist finder allows filtering by specialty and insurance, and the Headstrong Project (for post-9/11 veterans) covers costs entirely. Some states also fund veteran-specific mental health programs outside the VA — check your state's Department of Veterans Affairs website for what's available locally.

If cost is the barrier, ask any provider about sliding-scale fees. Many trauma-specialized therapists offer them. The Give an Hour network provides completely free sessions. The SAMHSA National Helpline (1-800-662-4357) can also help locate low-cost mental health services in your area.

Actionable Takeaway

If you haven't engaged with any mental health support since separation, start with the lowest-barrier entry point: download the PTSD Coach app today, or walk into your nearest Vet Center — no appointment required. If you're ready for more structured care, call the VA's Mental Health Crisis Line (not just for crisis — they can also connect you with outpatient services) at 988, Press 1. You don't have to be in a crisis to ask for help. See our full hotlines and escalation guide for a complete list of mental health contacts organized by situation.
