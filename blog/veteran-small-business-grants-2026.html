Veteran Small Business Grants You Can Apply for in 2026

Veterans launch small businesses at significantly higher rates than the general population — and the business community has responded with a growing ecosystem of grants, accelerators, and funding programs specifically for veteran entrepreneurs. The challenge is finding them. This guide covers the most accessible and substantial grant programs available to veteran-owned businesses in 2026, organized from federal programs through private foundation funding.

SBA Programs: The Starting Point

The Small Business Administration (SBA) doesn't offer direct grants to veteran-owned businesses, but it provides the infrastructure that makes other funding accessible. SBA's Boots to Business program — a free entrepreneurship curriculum offered in partnership with the military — is the on-ramp. Veterans who complete it gain access to mentorship networks, SBA loan programs with reduced fees, and connections to local Small Business Development Centers (SBDCs) where free business planning and grant application support is available.

The most useful SBA resource for veteran entrepreneurs is the Veterans Business Outreach Center (VBOC) network — 22 centers nationally that provide business plan assistance, loan packaging, and federal contracting guidance specifically to veteran entrepreneurs. VBOC services are free. If you're starting or scaling a business, contact your nearest VBOC before you spend money on consultants.

Grants.gov: Federal Opportunities Worth Monitoring

Federal grants for small businesses come through sector-specific agencies — not a central veteran small business grant program. The most significant are SBIR (Small Business Innovation Research) and STTR (Small Business Technology Transfer) grants, which fund research and development at small businesses. Phase I awards can reach $275,000; Phase II awards up to $1.75 million. These are competitive and technically demanding, but veteran-owned technology and manufacturing businesses that qualify are worth pursuing — veteran status doesn't provide preference, but the programs are open to all qualifying small businesses. Search grants.gov using NAICS codes matching your industry.

Hivers and Strivers: Angel Funding for Veteran Founders

Hivers and Strivers is an angel investment group that focuses exclusively on companies founded and led by veterans of the US military academies. Investments range from $250,000 to $1 million. It is not a grant — it is equity investment — but it is worth noting because it is one of the few veteran-specific early-stage funding sources of that size. If you graduated from a service academy and are building a scalable company, Hivers and Strivers is a direct path to capital from investors who understand the veteran entrepreneur profile.

StreetShares Foundation: Grants for Veteran Entrepreneurs

The StreetShares Foundation runs the annual Veteran Small Business Award, providing grants to veteran-owned businesses. Award amounts and application windows change annually — check streetsharesfoundation.com for current cycle information. The application is accessible and does not require a sophisticated grant writing background. If you have a functioning business with a clear impact story, this is worth adding to your application pipeline.

Amber Waves of Grain — and State-Level Programs

Several private foundations and corporations run veteran business grant competitions on annual cycles. The Warrior-Scholar Project, the Pat Tillman Foundation, and the Mission Continues all operate programs that intersect with veteran entrepreneurship at various points. State-level programs vary significantly: some states run dedicated veteran entrepreneur grant competitions or provide matching funds for veteran-owned businesses. Check your state's Department of Veterans Affairs economic development page and your state's Small Business Development Center network for state-specific opportunities.

Federal Contracting: The Larger Opportunity

Grants are valuable, but federal contracting is the larger long-term opportunity for most veteran-owned businesses. The federal government is legally required to award a percentage of its contracts to Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Businesses (SDVOSBs) and Veteran-Owned Small Businesses (VOSBs). These set-aside contracts are a structural advantage that is significantly underused by the veteran business community — often because certification and the procurement process feel complex. The SBA's Veteran Small Business Certification (VetCert) program is the entry point. Certification is free and creates access to a procurement market worth billions annually.

Actionable Takeaway

If you're starting a business: complete SBA Boots to Business, then contact your nearest VBOC for free business planning support. If you have an operating business: pursue Veteran Small Business Certification through the SBA VetCert program immediately — the contracting set-aside opportunities are more valuable than most one-time grants. If you're actively grant-hunting: set up a grants.gov account, search by NAICS code, and build a simple grant calendar tracking the StreetShares Foundation and any active state programs in your area.
